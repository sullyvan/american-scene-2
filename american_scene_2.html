Data Provenance

Numbered Agent Registry (21 agents)
Keyword vocabularies derived from: documented WPA / Federal Art Project program mandates (Smithsonian American Art Museum and National Archives public-domain records), LACMA collection-level tradition data (lacma.org/art/collection), and published movement scholarship. No agent encodes a named individual — every profile describes a program, a collection tradition, or a movement.

Curator Profiles (14 agents)
Derived from: LACMA departmental pages, publicly listed staff biographies, and department collecting mandates as described in LACMA's public communications. All living curators were contacted for opt-in prior to encoding. Departmental response voices are institutional in character — they represent the department's mandate, not the individual's personal opinion. Any curator may request removal or amendment; contact: sullivan.d.mike@gmail.com.

Cluster Definitions (8 art-tradition groupings)
Keyword sets constructed from LACMA's own collection taxonomy, augmented by terms from the Getty Art & Architecture Thesaurus (AAT) for each tradition. Published as open dataset Month 3 of Lab residency.

Images
Generated via Pollinations.ai (free, no-login API). No LACMA collection images are reproduced. All generated images are AI-produced and not attributed to any living or deceased artist without the "in the style of" qualifier standard in generative AI practice.

⚑ Disclaimer
This Lab project is designed to test these failures. All results will be published regardless of outcome per LACMA A+T safe-to-fail ethos. The three hypotheses below represent genuine risks to the project's central claim — that bridging-based distribution produces better civic art outcomes than engagement optimization.
1
Aesthetic Incoherence
High bridging score does not mean broad appeal — it may mean nobody likes it
▼
The Hypothesis
A work optimized for cross-tradition resonance may satisfy the scoring rubric while producing an image that is aesthetically incoherent — activating Social Realism keywords and Asian Art keywords and Conceptual keywords without belonging convincingly to any tradition. The bridging score measures semantic coverage, not aesthetic coherence. A muddled image can score 70 while a powerful, focused image scores 35. If users consistently experience high-bridge images as "confusing" or "trying too hard," the distribution model fails at its own civic purpose.
If We're Wrong — Falsification Metric
✗ Fail if
>60% of QR-scan users at community display nodes rate a high-bridge image (score ≥65) as "confusing" or "dislike" on the in-situ feedback prompt. Threshold measured across minimum 3 demonstration nodes in geographically distinct county regions and 40 scans per image before any result is reported.

2
Institutional Bias
The curator agent profiles may systematically disadvantage the traditions most relevant to LA's actual communities
▼
The Hypothesis
LACMA's curatorial structure reflects its acquisition history — which, like most encyclopedic museums, over-represents European modernism and abstract traditions relative to Social Realism, Latin American muralism, and community documentary. If the 14 curator agents carry this institutional bias in their keyword profiles, they will systematically reward works in the museum's collecting comfort zone and penalize the civic, labor-oriented, immigrant-community subjects that are most relevant to an LA County distribution network. The bridging score becomes a soft proxy for institutional prestige rather than genuine community resonance.
If We're Wrong — Falsification Metric
✗ Fail if
Curator agents collectively score works in WPA / Social Realism traditions more than 2 points lower (on the 0–10 agent resonance scale) than works in abstract / conceptual / decorative traditions, averaged across a minimum of 20 broadcast images spanning both categories. Measured at Month 3 when the dataset is large enough to run the comparison.

3
Geographic Polarization
A work can bridge art traditions without bridging county regions — the score may be blind to spatial inequality
▼
The Hypothesis
The bridging score operates on semantic keyword matching against 8 art-tradition clusters. It has no geographic awareness. A work could score 75 across the agent network while landing as deeply irrelevant in Wilmington, Pacoima, and Lancaster simultaneously — harbor, valley, and high-desert research contexts the county network is explicitly designed to test. A bridging score without spatial disaggregation is an aggregate abstraction that may mask exactly the kind of region-level polarization the WPA model was meant to overcome. The test therefore compares geographic difference across county regions — not only distance between ZIP codes — and must distinguish broad semantic bridging from actual countywide reach.
If We're Wrong — Falsification Metric
✗ Fail if
An image scores ≥60 overall on the bridging rubric but receives a node-level resonance score below 3/10 in three or more geographically distinct county regions, with at least one low-scoring node outside the Central Los Angeles region. Also flagged as failure: all strong responses come from geographically adjacent, museum-connected, or culturally similar nodes. Node-level scores derived from QR-scan ratings, dwell time, and repeat-scan rate. The comparison runs on regional difference across the county — not only ZIP-code distance — using the eight-node demonstration network.

Scoring Rubric
The actual formula used to compute each agent's resonance score
Step 1 — Build the Corpus
corpus = lowercase( theme + " " + printStyle + " " + artist.styleDescription )
Every broadcast assembles a single text string: the civic theme you entered, the chosen print style, and (if an artist agent is active) that artist's stylistic vocabulary — the same text used to prompt the image generator. The corpus is the work's full semantic fingerprint.
Step 2 — Keyword Match per Agent
firedKeywords = agent.keywords.filter(kw => corpus.includes(kw)) matchCount = firedKeywords.length maxPossible = agent.keywords.length
Each agent carries a keyword profile (10–20 terms) drawn from their known critical vocabulary, nationality, medium, and subject matter. A keyword "fires" when it appears anywhere in the corpus string. This is exact substring matching — no synonyms, no embeddings, intentionally legible and auditable.
Step 3 — Agent Resonance Score (0–10)
rawRatio = matchCount / maxPossible agentScore = min(10, round(rawRatio × 10 × 1.5))
The ratio is scaled by 1.5× to reward partial coverage (an agent who fires 4 of 12 keywords is meaningfully engaged, not absent). The score is capped at 10. This is the per-agent resonance shown in the table above.
Step 4 — Cluster Activation (for the Bridging Score)
clusterResonant = cluster.keywords.filter(kw => corpus.includes(kw)).length >= 1 resonatingClusters = count of resonant clusters (out of 8)
The 8 art-tradition clusters (Social Realism, Photography, Expressionism, Conceptual, Asian Art, Decorative Arts, Latin American, American Scene) each have their own keyword set. A cluster activates if at least one keyword fires.
Step 5 — Bridging Score (0–100)
basePct = resonatingClusters / 8 // breadth component avgDepth = mean( min(clusterMatchCount, 4) ) across resonant clusters depthBonus = (avgDepth / 4) × 0.20 // up to +20 pts for depth bridgingScore = min(100, round( (basePct × 0.80 + depthBonus) × 100 + (resonatingClusters >= 4 ? 5 : 0) ) // breadth bonus )
80% of the score rewards breadth — how many distinct traditions the work activates. 20% rewards depth — how many keywords fired within each activated cluster. A +5 point bonus applies when a work spans four or more clusters, reflecting the Bridging-Based Ranking framework's preference for cross-community reach over within-community depth.
Phase 2 Upgrade (Month 3 target)
agentScore = cosineSimilarity( embed(corpus), embed(agent.profileText) ) × 10
Keyword matching will be replaced with vector embedding similarity using the Claude API. Agent profiles will be converted to full prose descriptions, embedded once, and cached. The score semantics (0–10, capped) remain the same — only the matching engine changes. The open dataset will include both the keyword baseline and the embedding vectors so researchers can compare.

The Bridging Model

Unlike engagement algorithms that optimize within communities, bridging-based ranking rewards content that resonates across different groups. A work that appeals only to German Expressionists scores low. One that speaks to photographers, muralists, and printmakers alike scores high.

The Agent Network

21 numbered agents — seven WPA historical archive divisions, eight LACMA collection traditions, six contemporary American Scene movements — and 14 LACMA curators serve as resonance agents, each with a keyword profile drawn from program records, collection-level data, and departmental expertise. The bridging score is their collective response.

Long-Term Scale Hypothesis

At countywide or national scale, bridging would need to be demonstrated across regions, languages, local histories, environmental conditions, and forms of access — not inferred from a single aggregate score. The current eight-node network is a demonstration, not a deployment.

Data Provenance: Agent profiles derived from public LACMA collection tombstones, departmental pages, and staff listings as of Jan 2026. No living curator encoded without opt-in consent. Artist keyword profiles sourced from published catalogue essays and collection metadata.
